The Economics of Starting a VOD Service

The online television sector is booming and there's a lot of money to be made, right? Not so fast says Will Pitt of Geo Networks.
by Will Pitt 5 Feb 2014

In 1983 I was given my first Sony Walkman for Christmas, the revolutionary portable consumer device of its time. We carried them like weapons in the pockets of our baseball jackets and guarded them with our lives. That same Christmas saw the release of the first Now That's What I Call Music compilation and I don't think I spoke again for about three months (although Sarah Steel from two streets down tried to speak to me when she saw my headphones and she wouldn't even look at me before). I was the man. People went nuts for Walkmans and, just like today, every upgrade was met with anticipation and excitement. Remember anti-jog? How about Splash-proof?

The IPTV/VOD Market is Booming

Let's fast forward 30 years and I hope it's not too late to wish you all a Happy 2014! We know, because the market has reported, that Father Christmas delivered to many of you, young and not so young, a huge number of internet enabled devices (smartphones, tablets, PCs, games consoles, set-top boxes, Smart TVs etc.). Both Deloitte and the global information company IHS have reported on the extraordinary growth of consumer demand for these appliances. Indeed, Deloitte has suggested that as much as 50% of the UK consumer market now own a tablet such as Apple's iPad, Amazon's Kindle Fire or Samsung's Galaxy. It necessarily follows, therefore, that the early part of the year will see a huge upsurge in the demand for digital content. MarketsandMarkets states that the North American VOD market was worth over $21bn in 2013.

Everyone's Winning, Right?

For the broad range of technology companies who serve the IPTV market, this is great news, right? Well, possibly not. The gold rush is producing some surprising casualties. Recently there was news that the OTT video offering Vdio was shutting down as it was "…not able to deliver the differentiated customer experience we had hoped for".

Now, Vdio wasn't a start-up with an ill-thought out business plan and a couple of angel investors demanding a quick return. It was set up by Skype co-founder Janus Friis, hired Amazon's ex-head of global video Anthony Bay to run it and had in place content distribution agreements with all of the majors, including Disney, MGM, Paramount Pictures, Sony, Warner Bros, NBC Universal, Viacom, 20th Century Fox, Dreamworks  and others. It offered access to some incredible content.

Vdio also came from Rdio, its audio equivalent service that was launched in 2010 and is highly regarded.  Though subscriber numbers are elusive, Rdio provides access to over 20 million songs and provides services in 36 countries and they are thought to be challenging Spotify for market dominance. In other words, they're big, successful and, in relative terms, experienced.

Vdio offered every Rdio premium subscriber a $25 dollar credit on their pay-per-view service, which should have given it a tremendous kick-start. So why did it fail? And why have big industry names such as Amazon (which had planned to release a set-top box to enable users direct access to a VOD store) and Intel Media (which has been on the verge of releasing its set-top box and service 'OnCue' for the last two years and whom Verizon has just announced they are buying) begun to distance themselves from the consumer-facing TV market? The answer to both of those questions may lie in the simple 'supply and demand' business equation.

Supply and Demand

In a market place, if the ongoing and efficient supply of a product (content, in this case) increases, the price drops if there are a number of entities competing for market share. If we add to that scenario the fact that the content owners selling certain 'high-profile' content are able to charge something of a premium you can quickly see how non-content owners in a crowded market place might struggle, if there are a lot of places (platforms) for people to buy content.

A Vibrant IPTV/VOD Marketplace

Let's consider in basic terms what it is that creates a vibrant VOD market.

Step 1: Create content that people want;

Step 2: Create a route for that content to be consumed on any of the increasing myriad of devices out there. Not that long ago, as Apple's iTunes software, and the iTunes store, began to grow, many content owners chose to ignore, or attempted to sidestep them. This was a big mistake and was, in hindsight, incredibly naïve;

Step 3: Ensure that the route that the content takes (to the device) and the device itself make the consumer feel good. Is the experience reliable? Is the interface easy and fun to use? Does the interface encourage the consumer to view or listen to more by getting those first two points right as well as through recommendations?

A Sweet Analogy

Imagine a high street, which is lined with sweet shops. Many of these sweet shops sell the same products (because they're the most popular). Most of the shops don't actually play a part in producing the sweets, they just sell them on to their customers. These are the shops caught in a price war because there are a finite number of customers. This forces the shops to come up with more and more inventive ways to attract the customers;

  • Have a great shop front to draw the customers in
  • Make the shopping experience a delight
  • Create offers (buy a Curlywurly, get a Crunchie for half price)
  • Issue loyalty cards
  • Have promotion days etc.

But the shops that are owned or part-owned by the producers of the sweets will make more money because when customers buy their sweets, a greater proportion of the money spent will go to them and not to a third party.

Let's say one of these sweet shops - we'll call it NetSweets - is good at all of the above. It provides a great shopping experience, is well stocked with a broad variety of popular confectionery, is always open, has built up a loyal customer base and has started to produce its own sweets. It's a sweet shop that could do very well in this scenario, right? So what do the others do? Follow suit? Maybe.

Actually, a number of outcomes could follow in order to challenge NetSweets including buy-outs, joint ventures and consolidations. Would you open a new sweet shop here? You'd have to be brave and have a very compelling proposition. By the way, if you haven't already guessed, the VOD equivalent of NetSweets is Netflix and you could argue that Vdio is one of the shops that just battled in trying to attract customers along with all of the other competitors on the high street (some great, some good, some indifferent); and that's why they folded. 

Consumers Are Getting What They Want (for now)

Back to the VOD market. If these big hitters are launching platforms and then failing or stepping back, we could conclude that the marketplace has enough ways to get to the content that it wants.

If there are enough routes to market for the content to take and the consumers are satisfied with the price they're paying for it (and the margins being made by the providers of the content are acceptable and/or enough to enable growth - not always the case, by the way), what is the likelihood of success for new entrants?  

Technology Doesn't Stand Still

I hasten to add that technological developments don't reach a point and then stop (in any industry, not least the media and broadcast world) and that there could well be more game-changing platforms or devices around the corner. Whilst the growth of the IPTV/VOD market is undeniable, the majority of content isn't currently consumed by people using Smart TVs, tablets, games consoles or smartphones, but via their 'traditional' (sometimes not even HD) TVs (i.e. linear channels).  Just as, 30 years on from the launch of the Sony Walkman it's impossible to find someone who still owns one, in a further 30 years' time the only thing that's certain is that the market will look completely different again.  But focusing on the short to medium term, the trend that is most likely to emerge in 2014/15 is a raft of significant mergers and acquisitions because that is what often happens when relatively young markets begin to mature. One thing is for certain, it will be another fascinating and exciting year in this incredible industry that we are lucky to be a part of. Enjoy it!
